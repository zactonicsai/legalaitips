2. Documented AI Failure Cases

Mata v. Avianca, Inc. (2023) - The Landmark Case

New York Federal Court

What Happened: Attorney Steven Schwartz submitted a brief containing more than six fabricated case citations generated by ChatGPT.

Consequences: $5,000 fine, national media attention, and became the benchmark case for AI misuse in legal practice.

Key Lesson: The attorney admitted to using ChatGPT without understanding its limitations and failed to verify the cases existed.

Morgan & Morgan - Major Law Firm Sanctioned (2024)

#42 Law Firm by Size

What Happened: Attorneys at Morgan & Morgan (one of America's largest law firms) cited nine cases, but only one actually existed. The hallucinated cases came from an internal AI platform.

Consequences: Lead attorney fined $3,000 and removed from the case. Two other attorneys fined $1,000 each.

Key Lesson: Even large, sophisticated firms with internal AI systems are vulnerable. The firm stated: "This serves as a cautionary tale for our firm and all firms."

California Appeals Court - Record $10,000 Fine (2025)

Highest State Court Fine

What Happened: Attorney Amir Mostafavi filed a Social Security appeal where 21 of 23 quoted cases were fabricated by ChatGPT.

Consequences: $10,000 fine - the largest issued by a California state court for AI fabrications. The court published the opinion as a warning to all attorneys.

Key Lesson: The attorney claimed he didn't know ChatGPT would fabricate citations. Court stated: "No brief should contain citations that the attorney has not personally read and verified."

Johnson v. Dunn - Large Firm Supervisor Liability (2025)

Northern District of Alabama

What Happened: A practice group co-leader at a large, well-regarded firm inserted a hallucinated citation into a motion for leave to take a deposition.

Consequences: Court held that attorneys who sign pleadings are responsible for ALL content, regardless of who authored errors.

Key Lesson: Having a signature on a document creates absolute responsibility. "Stroke of pure luck" that the false citation supported an accurate legal statement.

Chicago Housing Authority - Double Irony (2025)

Cook County Circuit Court

What Happened: CHA attorneys cited the non-existent Illinois Supreme Court case "Mack v. Anderson" in a post-trial motion. The attorney had previously published articles on AI ethics.

The Irony: In the motion for sanctions against CHA, plaintiff's attorney also cited a hallucinated quote from "In Re Smith" - also generated by ChatGPT.

Consequences: Attorney relieved of position. Motion for sanctions filed. Demonstrates that even attorneys writing about AI ethics can fall victim.

U.S. District Court Arizona

What Happened: Attorney Cassandra Bam cited 12 fabricated cases out of 19 total citations in a brief "replete with citation-related deficiencies."

3. Proper Use Guidelines & Policy Framework

‚úì Fundamental Rules

  1. 1. NEVER submit AI-generated content without verification. Every citation, fact, and legal proposition must be personally verified.
  2. 2. Read the full source. Don't rely on AI summaries. Access and read the complete case, statute, or document.
  3. 3. Maintain professional responsibility. Your signature on a document means YOU are responsible for all content.
  4. 4. Protect client confidentiality. Never input privileged or confidential information into general AI tools.
  5. 5. Disclose AI use when required. Many courts now require disclosure of AI assistance in filings.

Court-Mandated Best Practices

Based on guidance from Madison County, Illinois and other jurisdictions:

Requirement Details
Accuracy Certification Certify that discovery answers are accurate, complete, and not generated solely by AI
Consent for Use All parties must consent to AI tool use in depositions and court proceedings
Privacy Restrictions AI tools must meet privacy restrictions and not store any information
Evidence Disclosure Disclose AI-generated evidence to opposing party 90 days before trial
Trial Identification Identify AI-generated evidence used at trial
Expert Disclosure Disclose expert's use of or reliance on AI in forming opinions

Verification Checklist

Before Using AI Output:

  • ‚òê Have I personally read all cited cases?
  • ‚òê Do the citations actually exist?
  • ‚òê Do the cases stand for the stated propositions?
  • ‚òê Is the legal authority still good law?
  • ‚òê Have I checked Shepard's or KeyCite?

Before Filing:

  • ‚òê Have I disclosed AI use if required?
  • ‚òê Is all factual information accurate?
  • ‚òê Are quotations exact and in context?
  • ‚òê Have I protected client confidentiality?
  • ‚òê Can I defend this filing's accuracy under oath?

4. AI Governance & Risk Controls

üõ°Ô∏è Why Governance Matters

Implementing robust AI governance and risk controls is essential for law firms and legal departments to harness AI's benefits while minimizing risks. These controls protect clients, maintain professional standards, and prevent the costly errors documented in hundreds of sanction cases.

1

Establish Clear Governance and Policies

Objective: Create a comprehensive AI usage policy that defines acceptable use, prohibited activities, and accountability measures.

Essential Policy Components:

  • ‚Ä¢ Approved Tools List: Specify which AI tools are authorized for firm use (e.g., Westlaw CoCounsel, Lexis+ AI, approved contract review platforms)
  • ‚Ä¢ Prohibited Tools: Explicitly ban general-purpose chatbots like ChatGPT for client work due to confidentiality and accuracy concerns
  • ‚Ä¢ Use Case Guidelines: Define when AI can be used (research, drafting, contract review) and when it cannot (final client advice without review)
  • ‚Ä¢ Accountability Structure: Designate an AI oversight committee or officer responsible for policy enforcement
  • ‚Ä¢ Disciplinary Measures: Establish consequences for policy violations, from warnings to termination
  • ‚Ä¢ Regular Updates: Commit to reviewing and updating policies quarterly as AI technology evolves

Real-World Impact: The Morgan & Morgan case demonstrated that even large firms can fail without clear policies. After their sanctions, they implemented comprehensive AI governance and training protocols.

2

Mandate Independent Verification of Output

Objective: Ensure every piece of AI-generated content is independently verified by a qualified attorney before use.

Verification Requirements:

  • ‚Ä¢ Citation Verification: Every legal citation must be independently located and read in full. Check that cases exist and support stated propositions
  • ‚Ä¢ Shepardize/KeyCite: Confirm all cited cases are still good law using Shepard's Citations or KeyCite
  • ‚Ä¢ Factual Accuracy: Cross-check all factual statements against source documents
  • ‚Ä¢ Legal Reasoning: Review AI's legal analysis for soundness and completeness
  • ‚Ä¢ Quotation Accuracy: Verify all quotations are exact and properly attributed
  • ‚Ä¢ Second Review for High-Stakes Work: Implement peer review for critical filings

Verification Workflow Example:

1
Attorney generates AI output
2
Different attorney reviews and verifies all citations
3
Reviewer signs certification of verification
4
Supervising attorney conducts final review
5
Document approved for filing/submission

Critical Reminder: In Johnson v. Dunn, the court held that signing a pleading makes you responsible for ALL content, regardless of who wrote it. Independent verification is your professional obligation.

3

Require Training for All Staff

Objective: Ensure all attorneys, paralegals, and staff understand AI capabilities, limitations, and proper use protocols.

Mandatory Training Curriculum:

  • Difference between general AI and legal-specific tools
  • Real-world failure case studies

  • ABA Resolutions 112, 700, and 604
  • State bar specific guidance
Module 3: Firm Policy & Procedures (1 hour)
  • Approved tools and prohibited tools
  • Verification workflows
  • Confidentiality protocols
  • Reporting and accountability
Module 4: Hands-On Practice (2 hours)
  • Using approved AI tools safely
  • Verification exercises with real examples
  • Identifying hallucinations in practice
  • Proper prompting techniques

Training Schedule:

  • ‚Ä¢ New Hires: Complete training within first 30 days of employment
  • ‚Ä¢ Current Staff: Initial comprehensive training immediately
  • ‚Ä¢ Refresher Courses: Quarterly updates on new developments and tools
  • ‚Ä¢ Annual Recertification: All staff must pass annual AI competency assessment
  • ‚Ä¢ Documentation: Maintain records of all training completion

Model Rules Connection: Rule 1.1 (Competence) requires lawyers to maintain knowledge and skill in their practice, including understanding the benefits and risks of relevant technology.

4

Restrict Input of Client Information

Objective: Protect attorney-client privilege and confidentiality by controlling what information can be entered into AI systems.

Strict Confidentiality Rules:

  • ‚ö† NEVER Input Into General AI Tools: No client names, case details, confidential information, or privileged communications in ChatGPT, Claude, or other general chatbots
  • ‚ö† Anonymization Required: When using AI for drafting or analysis, remove all identifying information (use "Client A," "Company X," generic party names)
  • ‚ö† Approved Tools Only: Use only AI tools with proper data protection agreements (DPAs) and security certifications
  • ‚ö† No Training on Client Data: Ensure AI vendors do NOT train models on your firm's data
  • ‚ö† Hypothetical Scenarios: Frame queries as hypothetical legal questions, not actual client situations

Security Requirements for Approved Tools:

Requirement Description
SOC 2 Type II Third-party audited security certification
Encryption Data encrypted at rest and in transit
GDPR/CCPA Compliance Meets privacy regulation requirements
Data Segregation Your data isolated from other customers
No Model Training Vendor contractually prohibited from training on your data
Data Retention Clear policies on how long data is stored

Client Consent Considerations:

  • ‚Ä¢ Some firms obtain explicit client consent before using AI tools on client matters
  • ‚Ä¢ Engagement letters may include AI use disclosure clauses
  • ‚Ä¢ Maintain transparency with clients about technology used in their representation

Model Rule 1.6 - Confidentiality: Lawyers have a duty to protect client information. Inputting confidential data into insecure AI systems violates this fundamental obligation and can result in discipline, malpractice claims, and loss of privilege.

5

Review Terms of Use and Privacy Policies

Objective: Understand vendor obligations, data handling practices, and legal protections before deploying any AI tool.

Critical Contract Terms to Review:

  • üìã Data Ownership: Who owns the data you input? Who owns AI outputs? Ensure your firm retains ownership
  • üìã Data Usage Rights: Can vendor use your data to train models? This should be explicitly prohibited
  • üìã Data Retention: How long is data stored? Can you request deletion? What happens after subscription ends?
  • üìã Data Breach Notification: Will vendor notify you of security incidents? What are response times?
  • üìã Third-Party Sharing: Does vendor share data with subprocessors? Who are they? What safeguards exist?
  • üìã Liability Limitations: What liability does vendor accept for errors? Are damages capped?
  • üìã Indemnification: Does vendor indemnify you for their tool's failures?
  • üìã Service Level Agreements (SLAs): Guaranteed uptime? Response time for issues?

Privacy Policy Red Flags:

üö® AVOID IF:

  • ‚Ä¢ "We may use your inputs to improve our models"
  • ‚Ä¢ "Data may be shared with third parties"
  • ‚Ä¢ Vague or unclear data retention policies
  • ‚Ä¢ No mention of encryption
  • ‚Ä¢ "We reserve the right to change terms at any time"
  • ‚Ä¢ No SOC 2 or equivalent certification

‚úÖ LOOK FOR:

  • ‚Ä¢ "Your data will never train our models"
  • ‚Ä¢ "Data is deleted upon request"
  • ‚Ä¢ Clear data segregation policies
  • ‚Ä¢ Enterprise-grade encryption specified
  • ‚Ä¢ GDPR/CCPA compliance statements
  • ‚Ä¢ SOC 2 Type II certification

Legal Department Review Checklist:

Best Practice: Never deploy an AI tool without a signed agreement that explicitly protects your firm's and clients' interests. Free tools may have unfavorable terms that expose you to liability.

8. Ethical Considerations & Bar Guidance

American Bar Association (ABA) Resolutions

The ABA periodically passes resolutions to provide guidance to lawyers and professionals. In recent years, three key resolutions address AI:

ABA Resolution 112 (August 2019)

Focus: Emerging ethical and legal issues in AI usage

Guidance: Urges courts and lawyers to address the emerging ethical and legal issues related to the usage of AI in the practice of law.

This foundational resolution recognized early on that AI would create novel ethical challenges for the legal profession.

ABA Resolution 700 (February 2022)

Focus: Pretrial risk assessment tools and bias prevention

Guidance: Urges governments to refrain from using pretrial risk assessment tools unless:

  • Data supporting risk assessment is transparent
  • Data is publicly disclosed
  • Tools are validated to demonstrate the absence of bias

This resolution addresses algorithmic bias in criminal justice applications of AI.

ABA Resolution 604 (February 2023)

Focus: Comprehensive AI system design and deployment guidelines

Guidance: Urges organizations that design, develop, deploy, and use AI systems and capabilities to follow several key guidelines including:

  • Ensuring transparency and accountability
  • Implementing appropriate safeguards
  • Addressing bias and fairness concerns
  • Maintaining human oversight and control
  • Protecting privacy and data security

Most recent and comprehensive resolution providing framework for responsible AI use across all aspects of legal practice.

State Bar Guidance

Multiple state bars have issued guidance on AI use:

  • California State Bar: Emphasized duty of supervision and verification
  • New York State Bar: Issued comprehensive AI ethics guidance
  • Florida Bar: Published guidelines on professional responsibility with AI

Federal Court Requirements

As of May 2024, over 25 federal judges have issued standing orders:

  • Disclosure of AI use in court filings
